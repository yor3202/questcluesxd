👨‍🦳 Baby Boomers (Born ~1946–1964)

Language shaped by post-war optimism, radio, and early TV

  • 1. “Disinterested”
    ✅ Used in formal writing
    🔍 Meant: Impartial or unbiased
    📍 Still confused today with “uninterested” (not caring)
  • 2. “Peruse”
    ✅ Common Use: “I’ll peruse the report.”
    🔍 Meant: Read carefully
    📍 Now often misunderstood as “skim quickly”
  • 3. “Retirement”
    🔍 Originally: A clear endpoint to a career
    📍 Now idealized or unattainable for many younger people
  • 4. “Groovy”
    ✅ Common Use: “That’s a groovy tune!”
    🔍 Meant: Cool or fashionable
    📍 Now retro, often used with irony
  • 5. “Long-distance”
    ✅ Use: “Make a long-distance call”
    🔍 Meant: Expensive phone calls across regions
    📍 Now outdated due to cell plans & internet
  • 6. “Far out”
    ✅ Used to express amazement
    🔍 Meant: Incredible or mind-blowing
    📍 Feels vintage or psychedelic today
  • 7. “Catch you on the flip side”
    ✅ Common on radio shows
    🔍 Meant: “See you later”
    📍 Obscure or humorous now
  • 8. “Solid”
    ✅ Use: “That’s a solid plan”
    🔍 Meant: Reliable, trustworthy
    📍 Still used, but origins feel dated
  • 9. “Square”
    ✅ Use: “Don’t be such a square.”
    🔍 Meant: Uncool, conventional
    📍 Now rarely used except jokingly
  • 10. “Right on”
    ✅ Use: “Right on, brother!”
    🔍 Meant: Agreement or support
    📍 Feels 60s/70s in tone now
